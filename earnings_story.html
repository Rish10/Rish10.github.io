Chapter One

Setting the Scene

Picture the floor of a theater where every actor must perform the same monologue, but with completely different material. The script is always the same ‚Äî revenue, earnings, guidance ‚Äî but the delivery, the subtext, and the audience reaction vary wildly. That's earnings season. And Thursday night was a full house.

By 4:05 PM Eastern, the New York Stock Exchange had closed its doors for the day, and the floodgates opened. One after another, press releases hit the wires. CEOs and CFOs dialed into earnings calls from their offices, hotels, and home studies. Analysts furiously updated their models. And somewhere on trading desks from Manhattan to Singapore, the machines began to react.

The broader backdrop mattered: the S&P 500 was sitting on a strong earnings season. Roughly 74% of companies that had reported so far in Q4 2025 had beaten expectations ‚Äî slightly below the historical average of 78%, but the magnitude of those beats was solid. Blended earnings growth for the index was running at 13.2% year-over-year, the fifth consecutive quarter of double-digit growth. Corporate America, broadly speaking, was healthy.

‚ö° The Macro Context

The next morning ‚Äî Friday the 13th ‚Äî would bring a January CPI print of 2.4% headline inflation, cooler than feared. That would give the whole market a sigh of relief. But on Thursday night, nobody knew that yet. Everyone was flying blind, armed only with their models and their gut.

Into this theater walked one hundred companies. Let's meet them.

Chapter Four

The Comeback Kids

Some of Thursday's most compelling stories weren't about companies hitting new highs ‚Äî they were about companies that had been written off, restructured, or beaten down, now showing signs of genuine life.

Diebold Nixdorf was bankrupt in 2023. By Thursday night in 2026, the ATM manufacturer was reporting earnings of $2.75 per share against an expectation of $1.65. The leaner, restructured version of the company ‚Äî freed from its debt burden ‚Äî was actually generating the profits that the old, overleveraged version never could. Sometimes bankruptcy is the best thing that can happen to a business.

West Pharmaceutical Services makes the little rubber stoppers and injection components that go into syringes and drug vials. Unexciting, right? Until you realize that GLP-1 obesity drugs like Ozempic and Wegovy require enormous quantities of injectable drug delivery components, and West Pharma makes them. Beat by 11.5%. The unglamorous beneficiary of the biggest pharmaceutical trend of the decade.

And yet ‚Äî the stock fell 15% after hours. Why? Because when analysts looked at the full-year 2026 guidance, they expected about $7.2 billion in revenue. DraftKings guided to $6.5‚Äì6.9 billion. The company was spending aggressively on a new "predictions" business (think: event contracts, like a legal sports prediction market). The future bet was real. Chapter Six

The Quiet Steadies

Not every company on Thursday night was trying to change the world or confessing a catastrophe. A large swath of corporate America simply did what it always does: showed up, delivered within a few cents of expectations, and went home. These are the companies that don't make headlines ‚Äî but they are, in many ways, the backbone of the economy.

The utilities ‚Äî American Electric Power, Exelon, Entergy, Fortis, Eversource, and PG&E ‚Äî all reported quietly, with results ranging from slightly better to roughly inline. The interesting subtext was that AI data centers are becoming a genuine demand tailwind for electricity. Every time someone runs a large language model, electrons flow somewhere. Those electrons come from utilities. AEP, in particular, is sitting in the middle of booming data center corridors in Ohio and Texas.

üí° The Hidden AI Story

AI's biggest beneficiaries aren't always the ones in the headlines. Utilities, industrial gas suppliers, cooling equipment makers, and fiber cable companies are all quietly riding the data center buildout. Thursday night's utility reports were Exhibit A.

The REITs told a similar story of quiet resilience. Kimco Realty's grocery-anchored shopping centers delivered exactly what was expected ‚Äî people still drive to the grocery store, regardless of what's happening in the economy. Iron Mountain ‚Äî which started life storing paper records in warehouses ‚Äî beat estimates modestly, underpinned by a growing data center business that now accounts for a significant chunk of its revenue. From paper filing cabinets to AI server racks. That's a journey.

Enbridge, the Canadian pipeline giant, put up a record year with $20 billion in annual EBITDA and raised its dividend for the 31st consecutive year. Thirty-one years. Through recessions, commodity crashes, pipeline protests, and a pandemic. The company also quietly disclosed $3 billion in data center-related power infrastructure for companies including Meta. Even pipelines are in the AI business now.

SPS Commerce, which runs the EDI network that connects retailers with their suppliers (think: the digital plumbing that tells a warehouse when Walmart needs more paper towels), beat by 13% and reported virtually zero customer churn. Nobody ever wrote a thrilling story about EDI software. But the companies that build essential, invisible infrastructure and charge for it forever are some of the best businesses in existence.

TransUnion, Morningstar, CBRE, US Foods, Ingersoll Rand, Lincoln Electric, and dozens of others added their voices to the chorus of "solid quarter, steady outlook." Not every story needs a dramatic arc. Sometimes reliability is its own form of excellence.

Chapter Seven

The Night's Wild Cards

And then there were the companies that defied easy categorization ‚Äî the ones whose stories were too strange, too surprising, or too contradictory to fit neatly into "beat" or "miss."

Vertex Pharmaceuticals technically missed EPS estimates by 2.3%, and yet nobody in the room was particularly worried. Vertex owns the cystic fibrosis drug market almost entirely ‚Äî there is no real competition. The miss came because the company is investing heavily in pain drugs, gene therapy for blood disorders, and a diabetes program. It's spending money to build the next Vertex while the current Vertex prints cash. A miss in service of a larger ambition.

Vale ‚Äî the Brazilian iron ore giant ‚Äî technically reported a loss per share versus an expected profit. But peeling back the layers, the core mining operations were fine. What killed the headline number was a cocktail of one-time charges, legal provisions from the Samarco dam disaster of 2015 (still haunting them, eleven years later), and currency fluctuations. The market shrugged ‚Äî not with approval, but with the weary familiarity of a Vale shareholder who's been through this before.

CRISPR Therapeutics reported a wider-than-expected loss, but their gene-editing therapy CASGEVY ‚Äî which can functionally cure sickle cell disease ‚Äî had just launched commercially. The losses were the cost of launching one of the most scientifically remarkable drugs in history. In ten years, if CASGEVY achieves its potential, this quarter's losses will look like seed money.

Epilogue

The Verdict

By midnight, the calls had ended. The press releases had been filed. The analysts had updated their models, the trading algorithms had made their first moves, and the CEOs had gone to bed ‚Äî some sleeping well, others staring at the ceiling.

What did Thursday night tell us about the world? A few things.

AI is real, and it's touching everything. Not just the obvious names ‚Äî the chip equipment makers, the network builders ‚Äî but the utilities carrying the electricity, the fiber laser makers cutting the metal for server racks, the REIT storing the data, the injectable drug companies whose components go into biotech processes that now run on AI. The economic tentacles of the AI buildout are longer than most people realize.

The consumer is bifurcating. Premium footwear brands (Birkenstock, Crocs) beat. Drive-through coffee (Dutch Bros) soared. Budget bread (Flowers Foods) cratered. The middle is hollowing out ‚Äî people are either treating themselves to something they love or trading ruthlessly down to the cheapest option. The brands caught in between are in trouble.

Tariffs are starting to show up in earnings. Pinterest named them directly. Other consumer-facing companies referenced cost pressure without spelling it out. The tariff effect is still early ‚Äî more of a ripple than a wave ‚Äî but Thursday night was one of the first times it showed up broadly across a single set of earnings.

Old-economy resilience is underappreciated. Enbridge's 31-year dividend streak. Agnico Eagle's century of gold production experience. Arista's boring but beautiful routing equipment. Lincoln Electric welding the world together. SPS Commerce connecting every retailer to every supplier via software that nobody glamorizes but nobody can live without. Thursday reminded us that the economy is mostly built by companies that never trend on social media.
